# 2. CLO MARKET: WHAT YOU NEED TO KNOW RIGHT NOW

## 2025 Was a Record Year
- US BSL + MM CLO new issuance: $203B (record second consecutive year)
- Repricing/refinancing: $315.7B (outstripped new issuance again)
- Combined activity: $546B (up from $511B in 2024)

## 2026 Issuance Outlook: $185-220B Expected
Forecasts vary. KBRA says $220B (10% increase). JPMorgan says $150B (20% decline). The middle ground is $185-200B. Either way, it's a big year.

European CLOs expected to hit record issuance too. €65B gross (8% uplift from 2025), with deal sizes trending from €400M to €500M benchmarks. 160-180 financing warehouses open as of November 2025. That's double the prior year. Pipeline is loaded.

## CLO Equity Arbitrage: Compressed but Workable
This is the question Nate will ask or expect you to understand. The answer:

Arb is tighter than historical levels. About 63% of bank loans trade above par, which limits par-build opportunities. Returns are driven by the IO (interest-only) component more than trading gains. Deal creation yields are in the low teens.

But "compressed" doesn't mean "broken." Managers are still printing deals. The refi/reset wave ($422B in CLOs exiting non-call periods in 2026) provides a technical tailwind for equity holders because they can refinance liabilities cheaper.

Your talking point for Nate: "Arb is tighter but workable. The bigger story is the $422B in non-call expirations this year creating reset opportunities. For a fund like SCF with control positions, that's an active management lever most allocators don't fully appreciate."

## Default Rate Outlook: Divergent Views
Major disagreement between rating agencies:

- Fitch: 4.5-5.0% by year-end 2026 (optimistic, expects rate relief to help levered issuers)
- Moody's: 7.5% by year-end (much more cautious)
- FTI survey: 77% of market participants expect defaults to increase vs. 2025

Current rates at two-year lows. But the forward view is uncertain. Sector risks concentrated in telecom, tech, and transportation.

Your position: Don't take a strong directional view. Acknowledge the divergence. Emphasize that SCF's diversification (236 positions, 83 managers) and multi-sector mandate provide cushion regardless of which scenario plays out.

## CLO Spread Levels (Current)
- US AAA: ~S+120-135 bps (consensus expects modest widening from current tights)
- European AAA: ~130 bps (tightened to 121-125 bps in early Feb with new investor entry)
- BBB: ~335 bps
- BB: ~550 bps for quality names

AAA CLO liabilities at 0th percentile since GFC. Historically tightest financing costs for equity investors. Good for existing positions being reset. Tougher for new issue economics.

## Base Rates and CLO Equity Distributions
SOFR declined from 4.3% to 3.7% in 2025. Further 50-100 bps of cuts expected in 2026.

The fear: Lower rates = lower equity distributions.
The reality: CLO equity is quasi-fixed-rate. Both asset yields and debt coupons float on SOFR. When SOFR drops, loan yields fall AND debt costs fall in parallel. The spread differential (which drives distributions) stays relatively stable.

This is a powerful rebuttal when allocators raise the "rates are coming down" concern. The structure naturally hedges the base rate component.

## CLO ETF Market: $35B+ and Accelerating
- Total AUM: $35B+ (doubled in just over a year)
- $4B in net inflows in first 6 weeks of 2026
- JAAA (Janus Henderson AAA CLO ETF): $27B
- Janus Henderson launched AA-A CLO ETF (ticker: JA) in February 2026
- Fidelity entered with FAAA and FCLO on February 12, 2026. Fee waiver for 12 months. Expense ratio 0.45%.

The ETF arms race validates your "CLO ETF-to-SCF funnel" thesis from the full context doc. Fidelity's entry makes this structural, not niche. Millions of investors now own CLO AAA tranches. The next question is "how do I get the 8-9% version?" and that's SCF.

## Manager Rankings (Context)
Top global CLO managers by AUM (Q3 2025): Blackstone Credit ($59B), Apollo (~$50B+), Carlyle ($48B). Significant consolidation activity. Manager tiering sharpening. Secondary market presence and pricing power becoming key differentiators.

Bain isn't the biggest. But your pitch isn't "we're the biggest CLO manager." It's "we're the only firm that is BOTH a CLO manager AND a CLO investor, across a multi-sector mandate, at institutional fees."

# 6. FIVE THINGS THAT CHANGE YOUR PITCH

1. **Lincoln fund is live.** Stop asking if it's real. Start asking how it's performing and where the structured credit allocation sits within it. The question becomes: "Does Lincoln's evergreen cover the wealth channel, or does SCF still need its own interval fund?"

2. **NAIC delay = extended sales window.** The RBC revision pushing to summer 2026 gives you a longer runway for the consulting-sale approach with insurance GAs. Every CIO is in limbo. Position SCF as the firm that can model outcomes across the full structured credit spectrum.

3. **BCC is now $61B with $30B+ in structured/liquid credit.** The platform story got bigger. Use it. Bain isn't a boutique CLO shop. It's a $61B credit platform with global origination, banking partnerships (SMBC), and infrastructure investments (Alter Domus).

4. **The interval fund land grab is real and accelerating.** VanEck, Oaktree, Blackstone, Neuberger, AMG BBH all launched or filed in the past 6 months. If Bain doesn't move, the structured credit interval fund slot gets taken by competitors with weaker track records but faster distribution execution.

5. **CLO equity arb is compressed but not broken.** Don't oversell the return environment. Acknowledge tight arb, lean into the reset/refi tailwind ($422B in non-call expirations), and emphasize that SCF's multi-sector mandate gives them the ability to rotate when CLO economics tighten further.
